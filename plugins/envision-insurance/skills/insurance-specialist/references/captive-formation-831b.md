# Captive Formation & the 831(b) Election — Qualification, Enforcement, and Compliance Architecture

> Evergreen reference for the insurance-specialist skill. Load-bearing figures verified 2026-07-04. For statute currency see references/current-legislation.md.

This file is decision support for forensic research, not legal or tax advice. Abuse patterns are documented so structures AVOID them: the framing throughout is "the IRS attacks X via Y; a defensible structure shows Z."

## 1. Captive fundamentals — 831(a) vs 831(b)

A captive is an insurance company owned by (or affiliated with) the insureds it covers, formed to insure the owners' own risks. For federal tax purposes a captive is only an "insurance company" if more than half its business is the issuing of insurance or annuity contracts or reinsurance (IRC § 816(a) definition, applied to non-life via § 831(c)) — and "insurance" itself is a judicial concept (Section 2 below). Everything downstream (premium deductibility under § 162, the 831(b) election, the 953(d) election) collapses if insurance status fails.

**IRC § 831(a) — default taxation.** A non-life insurance company pays corporate tax on its full taxable income: underwriting income (premiums earned minus losses and expenses, per subchapter L) plus investment income. Statute: 26 U.S.C. § 831(a) (https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/831, verified 2026-07-04).

**IRC § 831(b) — the small-company alternative tax.** An electing company is taxed ONLY on taxable investment income; underwriting/premium income is excluded from taxable income entirely. Two gates, both in § 831(b)(2)(A):
- **Premium cap** — net written premiums (or, if greater, direct written premiums) for the year must not exceed the inflation-indexed cap; and
- **Diversification requirements** — § 831(b)(2)(B) (Section 3 below).

**The cap, verified.** Base was set at $2,200,000 by the PATH Act for taxable years beginning in 2017, indexed for inflation in $50,000 increments (§ 831(b)(2)(D)).
- **Taxable years beginning in 2026: $2,900,000.** Rev. Proc. 2025-32, § 3.36 ("For taxable years beginning in 2026, under § 831(b)(2)(A)(i) the amount of the limit on net written premiums or direct written premiums (whichever is greater) is $2,900,000") — https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rp-25-32.pdf (verified 2026-07-04; corroborated by Captive Review). PINPOINT NOTE: a second verification pass (2026-07-04, 3-0) cited the same figure at § 4.36 — the dollar amount is confirmed twice; reconfirm the section pinpoint before external citation. Rev. Proc. 2025-32 reflects OBBBA (P.L. 119-21, enacted 2025-07-04) amendments.
- Taxable years beginning in 2025: $2,850,000 (Rev. Proc. 2024-40; corroborated by Captive Review — UNVERIFIED against the Rev. Proc. text itself, confirm before relying).
- Always re-verify the current-year cap from the latest annual inflation Rev. Proc.; never carry a prior year's figure forward.

**Election mechanics.** The election is made by the insurance company (not the insured) on its return (Form 1120-PC), applies to the year made and all subsequent years, and may be revoked only with the consent of the Secretary (§ 831(b)(2)(A) flush language). **Rev. Proc. 2025-13** (released 2025-01-13, alongside the final micro-captive regs) provides automatic consent procedures for revoking an 831(b) election — the standard unwind on-ramp (https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rp-25-13.pdf, existence and purpose verified via EY Tax Alert 2025-0359, 2026-07-04). Note the asymmetry in the final regs: a company that merely exceeds the premium cap or flunks diversification in a year is still a "Captive" for reportable-transaction purposes if the election remains in effect; only revocation removes it (EY 2025-0359, verified 2026-07-04).

**Why 831(b) is attacked.** The insured deducts premiums under § 162; the electing captive excludes those same premiums from income; family ownership of the captive can add estate-planning transfer. The IRS does not attack the statute — it attacks whether the arrangement is "insurance" at all, and since 2016 has layered reportable-transaction disclosure on top.

## 2. Insurance-status qualification

No Code definition of "insurance." Four-part judicial test (each prong independently fatal if failed):
1. **Risk shifting** — the insured's risk of economic loss actually moves to the insurer. Root: *Helvering v. LeGierse*, 312 U.S. 531, 539 (1941) ("Historically and commonly insurance involves risk-shifting and risk-distributing") — https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/312/531/ (verified 2026-07-04).
2. **Risk distribution** — the insurer pools a sufficiently large number of independent risk exposures that the law of large numbers operates. This is the prong micro-captives most often fail (every case in Section 5).
3. **Insurance risk** — fortuity; not investment risk, timing risk, or business risk repackaged.
4. **Commonly accepted notions of insurance** — the arrangement looks and behaves like insurance: valid organization and regulation, adequate capitalization, arm's-length premiums actuarially determined, timely-issued unambiguous policies, real claims administration, no circular cash flow.

**Safe harbors (2002 revenue rulings)** — the IRS's own map of defensible structures (all verified 2026-07-04):
- **Rev. Rul. 2002-89** (https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rr-02-89.pdf): parent-captive arrangement qualifies where the captive derives ≥50% of premiums and risk exposure from unrelated third parties; fails at 10%. Compare *Harper Group v. Commissioner*, 96 T.C. 45 (1991) (≈30% unrelated business sufficed) — the case-law floor is lower than the ruling's safe harbor.
- **Rev. Rul. 2002-90** (https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rr-02-90.pdf): brother-sister risk distribution — captive insuring 12 operating subsidiaries, each contributing 5%–15% of total risk, qualifies. This is the standard architecture for a single-family-of-companies captive.
- **Rev. Rul. 2002-91** (https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rr-02-91.pdf): group captive owned by a significant number of unrelated insureds (7 in the ruling, none >15% of risk) qualifies.

**Risk pools.** Many micro-captive promoters manufactured "risk distribution" by running each client captive's premium through a pooled reinsurance entity that cross-reinsured all clients. Courts have treated these pools as shams where premiums circled back dollar-for-dollar, pool pricing was not actuarial, and the pool paid essentially no claims (*Avrahami*, *Reserve Mechanical*, Section 5). A pool is not per se invalid — but a defensible pool shows genuinely uncorrelated third-party risk, actuarial pricing, real claims payment, and non-circular cash movement.

## 3. § 831(b)(2)(B) diversification requirements (PATH Act, effective taxable years beginning after 2016)

An electing company must satisfy ONE of two tests each year (statute: 26 U.S.C. § 831(b)(2)(B), https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/831, verified 2026-07-04):

1. **20% single-policyholder gross-premium test** — no more than 20% of net written premiums (or direct written premiums, if greater) for the year is attributable to any one policyholder. Related policyholders aggregate: two or more policyholders related within § 267(b) or § 707(b), or members of the same § 831(b)(2)(B)(ii)-referenced controlled group, count as one policyholder.
2. **Ownership/relatedness alternative test** — available when the 20% test fails (the classic one-operating-business captive). No "specified holder" may hold (directly or indirectly) aggregate interests in the captive that exceed, by more than a **de minimis** percentage, that holder's percentage interest in the insured business or its assets. A specified holder is an individual who holds an interest in the captive and who is a **spouse or lineal descendant** (including by adoption) of an individual holding an interest in the specified insured business. "De minimis" is undefined in the statute; legislative history and IRS guidance treat a spread of **2 percentage points or less** as de minimis (guidance-level, not statutory — confirm current IRS position before relying).

**The pattern the PATH Act targeted:** business owner deducts premiums paid to a captive owned by the owner's spouse, children, or a dynasty trust for their benefit — converting deductible "premiums" into a wealth transfer that bypasses gift and estate tax. Under the ownership test, the captive's ownership must mirror the insured's ownership (within the de minimis band) whenever spouses/lineal descendants are in the cap table. A defensible single-insured captive shows congruent ownership between insured and captive; an estate-freeze-shaped captive fails the test and loses the election.

## 4. IRS enforcement arc: Notice 2016-66 → CIC Services → T.D. 10029 → 2026 vacatur litigation

**Notice 2016-66 (Nov. 1, 2016).** Designated micro-captive arrangements as "transactions of interest": 831(b) captive + ≥20% related ownership + (financing factor over most-recent-5-year period OR loss ratio <70%). Retroactive disclosure to Nov. 2, 2006.

**CIC Services, LLC v. IRS, 593 U.S. 209 (2021).** Unanimous SCOTUS (Kagan, J., decided May 17, 2021): the Anti-Injunction Act does not bar a pre-enforcement APA suit against Notice 2016-66's reporting mandate, because the suit targets the reporting obligation, not tax assessment/collection (https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/20pdf/19-930_k5fm.pdf, verified 2026-07-04). On remand, the E.D. Tenn. **vacated Notice 2016-66** (opinion Mar. 21, 2022, 592 F. Supp. 3d 677) for APA notice-and-comment failure and an inadequate administrative record.

**Proposed regs (Apr. 11, 2023, REG-109309-22).** Obsoleted Notice 2016-66; proposed a listed-transaction tier (loss ratio <65% OR financing factor — disjunctive) and a TOI tier.

**T.D. 10029 — final regulations (published 90 Fed. Reg., Jan. 14, 2025)** (https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/01/14/2025-00393/micro-captive-listed-transactions-and-micro-captive-transactions-of-interest; factor details verified against EY Tax Alert 2025-0359, both 2026-07-04). "Captive" = entity that (1) has an 831(b) election in effect, (2) issues/reinsures contracts for an Insured, and (3) has ≥20% of assets, voting power, or value owned by an Insured, an owner of an Insured, or related persons (the relationship test).

- **Listed transaction — Treas. Reg. § 1.6011-10(c).** CONJUNCTIVE — the Captive must have BOTH:
  - **Financing factor**: during the Financing Computation Period (defined at § 1.6011-10(b) — the commonly cited "most recent 5 taxable years" pin FAILED adversarial verification 1-2 on 2026-07-04; confirm the period length against the reg text before quoting; entire existence if shorter), the Captive directly or indirectly financed or conveyed (or agreed to) a guarantee, loan, or other transfer of its capital to a related recipient in a transaction that did not produce taxable income or gain to the recipient — including pre-period financing still outstanding in the disclosure year; AND
  - **Loss ratio factor**: liabilities for insured losses + claim administration expenses over the Loss Ratio Computation Period (**the Captive's 10 most recent taxable years**) are **less than 30%** of premiums earned (net of policyholder dividends) over the same period. (Final regs cut this from the proposed 65% and made the test conjunctive — a deliberate narrowing.)
  - Because the listed tier requires a 10-year loss history, a captive under 10 years old cannot be listed — at most a TOI.
  - Exclusions: certain employee-compensation coverage captives and "Seller's Captives" (dealer/lender/retailer/service-provider captives insuring products sold to unrelated customers).
- **Transaction of interest — Treas. Reg. § 1.6011-11(c).** DISJUNCTIVE — Captive has the financing factor during its financing computation period OR a loss ratio **below 60%** (the band ≥30% but <60% without financing, or <30% without a financing factor / without 10-year history). Loss ratio ≥60% falls outside both tiers even with a financing factor. TOI computation period: most recent 10 taxable years or entire existence if shorter.
- **Disclosure duties.** Participants (Insureds, Owners, the Captive, Intermediaries): Form 8886 with each year's return under § 6011/Treas. Reg. § 1.6011-4, plus copy to the Office of Tax Shelter Analysis on first filing. Prior-year participation with an open assessment limitations period as of Jan. 14, 2025: disclose within 90 days (i.e., by ~Apr. 14, 2025). Material advisors: Form 8918 under § 6111 (quarterly deadline), disclosure required if a tax statement was made within 6 years before Jan. 14, 2025; § 6112 investor-list maintenance (produce within 20 business days of IRS demand; § 6708 penalty $10,000/day thereafter).
- **Penalty stack.** § 6707A participant nondisclosure: 75% of the tax decrease, capped at $200,000/$100,000 (entity/natural person) for listed, $50,000/$10,000 for other reportable; minimums $10,000/$5,000; no rescission authority for listed. § 6662A reportable-transaction understatement penalty (20%/30%). § 6707 material-advisor nondisclosure ($50,000; for listed, the greater of $200,000 or 50% of gross income derived — 75% if intentional). **§ 6700 promoter penalty** for organizing/selling an abusive plan with false or fraudulent tax statements: 50% of gross income derived from the activity — the IRS's promoter-audit weapon against captive managers; DOJ injunction suits ride alongside.
- Revoking the 831(b) election (Rev. Proc. 2025-13) removes "Captive" status prospectively from the effective year of revocation, ending participant status for those years absent a successor captive (T.D. 10029 text, verified 2026-07-04).

**2025–26 litigation over the final regs (both verified 2026-07-04):**
- ***Drake Plastics Ltd. Co. v. IRS*, No. H-25-2570 (S.D. Tex. Apr. 16, 2026)**: court **vacated Treas. Reg. § 1.6011-10 (the listed-transaction tier) universally** — § 6707A(c)(2) requires listed transactions to be presumptively tax-avoidant, and the IRS showed only that the covered transactions share features typical of abusive deals, not that transactions with those features are typically abusive; vacatur stayed to May 1, 2026. The **TOI tier (§ 1.6011-11) was upheld** — the "potential for tax avoidance" standard of § 6707A(c)(1) was satisfied by the Tax Court record. (Parker Tax Pub. May 2026; Current Federal Tax Developments 2026-04-17.)
- ***Ryan, LLC v. IRS* (N.D. Tex., decision reported 2026-07-01; exact opinion date UNVERIFIED)**: separately **upheld § 1.6011-11** as neither arbitrary nor capricious — Treasury's administrative record (Tax Court decisions, exam data, NAIC industry loss-ratio statistics, notice-and-comment record) distinguished it from the vacated Notice 2016-66; the listed-transaction challenge was moot after *Drake Plastics* (captive.com, 2026-07-01).
- **Net state as of 2026-07-04:** TOI disclosure (Form 8886/8918 under § 1.6011-11) remains fully in force, twice sustained. The listed-transaction tier is vacated nationwide at district-court level. Whether the government appeals *Drake Plastics* or re-promulgates with a stronger record: NOT FOUND as of verification date — check before relying. Substantive audit exposure (deduction disallowance, penalties) never depended on the reporting regs and is unaffected by the vacatur.

## 5. Case-law abuse-pattern synthesis

Each case: holding, then the specific factors the court keyed on. These factors are the audit playbook — a defensible captive is built to show the opposite of each.

**Avrahami v. Commissioner, 149 T.C. 144 (2017)** (first micro-captive Tax Court opinion; https://www.ustaxcourt.gov, verified via IRS/EY secondary sources 2026-07-04). St. Kitts captive ("Feedback," 953(d)-elected) insuring the owners' jewelry stores and shopping centers; risk distribution claimed through the Pan American terrorism cross-insurance pool. Held: no risk distribution and not insurance in the commonly accepted sense; premium deductions denied. Factors: (a) circular cash flow — pool premiums returned to related entities nearly dollar-for-dollar; (b) implausible actuarial pricing — insurance spend jumped from ~$150K commercial to ~$1.3M captive premiums engineered to approach the then-$1.2M cap; terrorism policy priced for a risk the actuary could not defend; (c) essentially no claims until audit began; (d) premium dollars looped back to the owners as unsecured "loans" (the Belly Button Center transfers); (e) policies ambiguous, late-issued.

**Reserve Mechanical Corp. v. Commissioner, 34 F.4th 881 (10th Cir. May 13, 2022), aff'g T.C. Memo. 2018-86** (https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca10/18-9011/18-9011-2022-05-13.html, verified 2026-07-04). Anguilla captive for an Idaho mining-equipment company; risk distribution claimed through the PoolRe quota-share pool. Held: no risk distribution (pool was circular — each participant's "third-party" premium was matched to reinsurance of its own risk), premiums not arm's length, not insurance. **953(d) consequence:** because Reserve was not an insurance company, its § 953(d) election was invalid; it was therefore a foreign corporation, and its premium receipts were U.S.-source FDAP income taxed at 30% under § 881(a) — no deductions allowed (holding confirmed in IRS CCA 202422010, https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-wd/202422010.pdf, verified 2026-07-04). Factors: circular pool flows, premiums reverse-engineered near the cap, near-zero claims, policies issued late with boilerplate terms, no genuine underwriting.

**Syzygy Insurance Co. v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2019-34.** Delaware captive with fronting-carrier pool layers. Held: no risk distribution through the fronting arrangements and not insurance in the commonly accepted sense; the 831(b) election was invalid and insured-level deductions denied — but the court declined § 6662 accuracy penalties for reasonable cause / good-faith reliance on advisors. Factors: pool that did not truly distribute risk, policies with vague and conflicting terms issued after coverage periods began, premium pricing unsupported by claims experience, claims handling informal. (Citation stable; details verified via secondary practitioner sources 2026-07-04.)

**Caylor Land & Development, Inc. v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2021-30.** Anguilla captive for an Arizona construction group. Held: no risk distribution (the "12 insureds" were functionally one construction business; no viable pool) and not insurance in the commonly accepted sense; and — the enforcement inflection point — the court **sustained § 6662 accuracy-related penalties**, rejecting reliance-on-advisor defenses where the advisors were the promoters. Factors: zero claims filed until the audit, premiums spiking to just under the cap, no arm's-length pricing, conflicted advice. After Caylor, penalty protection via "my captive manager said it was fine" is effectively dead; independent counsel/actuarial opinions are the only reliance with weight.

**Cross-case synthesis — the recurring abuse factors:** (1) circular cash flow (premium → captive/pool → loan-back or matched reinsurance to the payor); (2) premiums reverse-engineered to the 831(b) cap rather than to actuarial exposure; (3) no or trivial claims activity over multiple years (the fact pattern the T.D. 10029 loss-ratio factor mechanizes); (4) sham risk pools with correlated or matched risk; (5) policies late, ambiguous, or covering implausible risks; (6) promoter-controlled "independence" (same firm forms, manages, prices, and defends the captive).

## 6. § 953(d) offshore election mechanics

**What it does.** A controlled foreign corporation that would qualify as an insurance company under subchapter L may elect under IRC § 953(d) to be treated as a **domestic** corporation for all Code purposes: files Form 1120-PC, can make the 831(b) election, avoids the § 4371 foreign-insurer excise tax (4% direct property/casualty premiums, 1% reinsurance) and CFC/subpart F complexity for its U.S. owners.

**Mechanics.** Election statement filed with the IRS per Rev. Proc. 2003-47; the electing company must **enter a closing agreement** with the IRS committing to tax compliance, and must either maintain a U.S. office/assets sufficient to meet tax obligations or post a letter of credit as security. Election is irrevocable without consent; once terminated, a 5-year re-election bar applies (§ 953(d)(2)). (Mechanics stable law; Rev. Proc. 2003-47 pinpoint UNVERIFIED this session — confirm before relying.)

**Failure mode (the Reserve Mechanical trap).** The election is only available to a company that IS an insurance company. If the captive's arrangements are held not to be insurance, the 953(d) election is **invalid ab initio**: the entity reverts to foreign-corporation status; premium receipts become U.S.-source FDAP taxed at a flat 30% under § 881(a) with **no deductions**; § 4371 excise exposure on the premiums revives; and information-reporting failures (Form 5471, § 6038) can cascade. This double penalty — deduction denied at the insured AND gross-basis 30% tax at the captive — is why offshore micro-captives fare worse in litigation than domestic ones. IRS CCA 202422010 (2024) extends the abusive-micro-captive analysis to foreign entities on exactly this path (verified 2026-07-04).

**Bermuda/Cayman interplay.** Neither Bermuda nor Cayman has a comprehensive U.S. income-tax treaty, so there is no treaty rate reduction on the § 881 30% tax if the election fails — offshore domicile provides regulatory flexibility, not a tax backstop. Small captives in these domiciles (and St. Kitts, Anguilla, Nevis — the promoter-era favorites) almost always run 953(d) + 831(b) in tandem; both elections stand or fall together on insurance status. Domestic domiciles (Vermont, Utah, Tennessee, Delaware, etc.) avoid the § 881/§ 4371 cliff entirely, which is one reason post-enforcement formations have migrated onshore.

## 7. Structure-around checklist (the compliance payoff)

A captive that intends to survive exam demonstrates, with contemporaneous documentation:

- **Genuine risk transfer** — real, fortuitous, historically evidenced exposures; coverage gaps in commercial policies identified in a feasibility study BEFORE formation; no policies for risks the business has never faced and cannot plausibly face.
- **Arm's-length actuarial pricing** — premiums set by an independent credentialed actuary from exposure data, re-priced annually; premium totals that float with exposure, not premiums parked just under the 831(b) cap year after year (the cap-hugging pattern is a named factor in every lost case).
- **Real claims history** — claims are noticed, adjusted, documented, and paid or denied on policy terms; a multi-year zero-claims record is now a regulatory tripwire (T.D. 10029 loss-ratio factor) as well as a litigation factor. Do not manufacture claims — write coverage the business actually uses.
- **Independent management** — captive manager, actuary, and auditor independent of the promoter and of each other; a functioning board that meets and minutes; the tax opinion from counsel with no economic stake in formation fees (the Caylor penalty lesson).
- **Capital genuinely at risk** — real capitalization meeting domicile minimums with margin; captive assets invested like an insurer's (liquid, matched to reserves), not in illiquid affiliate deals.
- **No circular loan-backs** — no loans, guarantees, or capital conveyances from captive to insureds/owners/related parties. This is simultaneously the T.D. 10029 financing factor (financing-computation-period lookback plus outstanding-balance carry-forward — confirm period length at § 1.6011-10(b)) and the Avrahami cash-circle factor. If historical loans exist, unwind and document repayment before year-end.
- **Risk distribution that maps to a safe harbor** — ≥12 brother-sister insureds each 5–15% of risk (Rev. Rul. 2002-90), or ≥50% unrelated risk (Rev. Rul. 2002-89), or a genuine group captive (Rev. Rul. 2002-91). If a pool is used: verify uncorrelated risk, actuarial pool pricing, actual pool claims payment, and no premium matching.
- **Diversification-test compliance file** — annual computation of the 20% policyholder test with § 267(b)/707(b) aggregation, or the ownership-mirror test with cap tables for both insured and captive; flag any spouse/lineal-descendant holder immediately.
- **Reportable-transaction hygiene** — compute the loss-ratio and financing factors annually against current regs; file Form 8886 when within the TOI band (§ 1.6011-11 is in force and twice sustained); calendar material-advisor obligations if the org advises third parties.
- **Contemporaneous documentation** — policies issued before coverage periods begin, unambiguous terms, board minutes, feasibility study, actuarial reports, claims files. Courts weight what existed at the time, not what was assembled for audit.

## 8. Settlement / unwind landscape

**First initiative (announced Sept. 16, 2019, IR-2019-157)** (https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/irs-offers-settlement-for-micro-captive-insurance-schemes-letters-being-mailed-to-groups-under-audit, verified 2026-07-04): time-limited offers to taxpayers under exam — substantial concession of claimed benefits (90% premium-deduction disallowance) plus reduced penalties. IRS reported ~80% acceptance among offerees; simultaneously stood up 12 dedicated micro-captive exam teams.

**Second, stricter initiative (announced Oct. 22, 2020, IR-2020-226)** — terms per Caplin & Drysdale analysis in Captive Review (verified 2026-07-04): 100% disallowance of premium deductions and captive-related expenses in open years; in exchange, the captive does NOT recognize premium income and § 1442/1461 withholding is treated as satisfied; mandatory captive liquidation within 90 days OR a deemed qualified dividend approximating full distribution (basis adjustments prevent double tax); accuracy penalty at 15%, reducible to 10% (no-other-reportable-transaction declaration) or 5% (independent-advisor reliance declaration); one $5,000 § 6707A penalty if never disclosed; gift-tax returns required where trusts own the captive; the 831(b) and 953(d) elections terminate after the last settled year; Form 906 closing agreement; no counter-offers; ineligible once docketed in Tax Court.

**Current status (as of 2026-07-04).** No currently open, generally available IRS micro-captive settlement initiative was found in this verification pass — the 2019/2020 programs were offer-letter-gated and time-limited. UNVERIFIED whether case-by-case Exam/Appeals resolutions on similar frameworks remain available; confirm current IRS posture before advising. Outside settlement, expect: 100% deduction disallowance, 20% § 6662 or 40% § 6662(i) undisclosed-non-economic-substance penalties, § 6707A penalties if the arrangement was disclosable and undisclosed, and (offshore) the § 881 gross-basis exposure.

**Exit and unwind considerations:**
- **Election revocation** — Rev. Proc. 2025-13 automatic-consent path; revocation ends "Captive" status (and participant status) prospectively from its effective year under T.D. 10029, provided no successor captive is established.
- **Disclosure tail** — TOI disclosure obligations under § 1.6011-11 survive the *Drake Plastics* vacatur (which reached only the listed tier) and were re-affirmed in *Ryan, LLC*; unwinding does not erase prior-year filing duties for open years.
- **Liquidation tax mechanics** — § 331 liquidation gain at shareholder level on accumulated surplus; deferral of closed-year premium benefit ends at distribution; model before choosing revoke-and-run-off vs. liquidate vs. convert to 831(a) taxation.
- **Run-off alternative** — a captive with genuine insurance operations can revoke 831(b), pay tax under 831(a), and keep operating; this preserves legitimate risk financing while exiting the enforcement perimeter.
- **Trust-owned captives** — expect gift-tax scrutiny on unwind (a required workstream in the 2020 settlement terms); brief transfer-tax counsel early.
- **Statute management** — assessment limitations may remain open (§ 6501(c)(10) holds the statute open for undisclosed listed transactions until disclosure; effect of the listed-tier vacatur on § 6501(c)(10) exposure for pre-vacatur years: UNVERIFIED — confirm before relying).
